02 The Equity You've Built

Three Layers, Two Strong, One To Evolve

Two are earned and working. The third is the one this audit is really about.

Operator equity. ModernMD has spent eleven years building a real urgent care operation in the hardest healthcare market in the country. 13 communities across Brooklyn, Queens, and the Bronx, no Manhattan. A joint venture structure with The Brooklyn Hospital Center alongside Blue Wolf Capital and Advantage Capital. A CEO who arrived in 2024 from a company he scaled to 6,500 partner sites. A professionalized bench across operations, medicine, talent, sales, and finance. The framing in the LinkedIn About line, "re-defining the healthcare delivery system in your community," is not aspirational copy. It is the actual business model, with a community-hospital partner sitting in the cap table to back it up.

Audience equity. The owned channels are real and consistent. 2,468 followers on the company LinkedIn. Roughly 639 on Instagram with 390 posts. 356 likes on Facebook. The active brand voice ("Hustle Without the Hurry," "Quality Care. Modern Convenience.") is recognizable and ownable. The publishing system has been running for years, the visual identity is consistent, and the team has clearly been doing the work to keep the channels alive. The numbers are modest, but the habit of showing up is in place.

Storytelling equity. This is the layer worth evolving. There is no long-form video presence anywhere on a ModernMD-owned surface. The YouTube channel that does exist (@modernmd7603) carries two 31-second commercial spots from three and four years ago, with 77 combined views, and has been dormant since. There is no homepage video. There is no patient-story format, no doctor-led explainer, no neighborhood profile, no series of any kind. The LinkedIn post mix is internal-celebration. The Instagram is photo-heavy. The strongest narrative ModernMD owns (outer-borough family clinics, a JV with a community hospital, thirteen neighborhoods that the larger urgent-care chains do not anchor in the same way) is told as ad copy, not as film. And the most audience-ready voice in the entire ModernMD orbit, your published voice on measurement and the ninety-nine percent of marketing that isn't working, lives on Medium and a personal book site rather than on ModernMD's owned channels.

That gap is what this audit is really about. The hard part, building the clinics, the team, the operating rhythm, and the performance machine, is either done or being done right now. The work below is on the brand layer that compounds on top of it.

03 Lead With Value

The Legendeering Framework, In One Page

The methodology behind every Talex production. Five directives, one core principle.

Legendeering is Talex's proprietary strategy. The core principle is straightforward: lead with value, always. The brand earns trust by giving without expectation, and that equity compounds because people keep choosing to watch.

Five directives drive it:

  • ·Give Without Expectation. Value delivered without strings. The kind of generosity that builds loyalty money cannot buy.
  • ·Be Relentlessly Authentic. Say true things. Show real work. Speak like a human being.
  • ·It's Not About You. It's About Them. The audience has to come first. It's all about their wants and needs and making something they will choose to watch.
  • ·Start With Story, Follow With Facts. Story gives information shape, context, and emotional weight. Facts alone rarely move people.
  • ·Consistency Is Key. Trust is built in moments, repeated over time.

Each format below is one of these directives in production. "What The Borough Walks In With" is relentlessly authentic, because the cast is patients and staff who already live in the neighborhood. "After Hours" starts with story before any clinical fact. And "The Honest Cost of Healthcare" puts the audience first in the most direct way possible: every episode answers a question the audience actually asked.

ModernMD has built genuine operator equity and a real publishing habit. The three formats below are what that equity looks like when it consistently lands as story instead of as ad copy.

04 Three Ideas For Video

Formats That Fit The Borough Story

Each idea passes the four tests: audience-first, entertaining as a stand-alone watch, novel rather than a remix, and built to compound across episodes. None of them require a new audience. They just need a home.

01
"What The Borough Walks In With"
A returning long-form documentary series. Each episode profiles one ModernMD clinic and the neighborhood around it. The cast is patients and staff: a doctor at one of the Crown Heights or Bedford-Stuyvesant locations who has been there for years; a family who comes in for camp physicals, ear infections, and the grandfather's chest pain; a medical assistant who lives a few blocks from the clinic she works at. The neighborhood is the cast. ModernMD is the bridge between people and care, not the hero. The Brooklyn Hospital JV and the thirteen-community footprint make the framing structurally true rather than copywriter-true. The series compounds because every clinic is one episode, the work in the neighborhood is already happening, and the audience finds the show by searching for their own neighborhood.
02
"After Hours"
A returning short documentary series filmed during the hours a primary care office is closed: weekday evenings, weekends, and holidays. Each episode follows one shift through the eyes of a doctor, PA, or front-desk lead. The hook is that urgent care is the only thing open at 9pm on a Sunday in Bushwick or Flatbush, and the medical-cinema slot for that fact has never been filled. The format directly maps to the brand line, "Hustle Without the Hurry." Audience: anyone who has ever been to urgent care after hours, which in NYC is most of the city. The series compounds because every shift is a self-contained story and the hook works year-round.
03
"The Honest Cost Of Healthcare"
A first-person POV series with you as the host. You have publicly written that ninety-nine percent of marketing isn't working; the same skepticism applied to healthcare costs, surprise bills, what insurance actually covers, what an urgent-care visit actually costs versus what an ER visit costs. Each episode answers one real question, sourced from ModernMD comments, Reddit r/AskNYC, and patient feedback. Honest answers, no marketing voice. The format takes your published voice, the voice on Medium and on the book site, and runs it through ModernMD's owned channels. The audience submitting questions is the audience telling you what they care about, which is the most measurable signal long-form video produces.
